Worked example

WALK THE
INCIDENT.

// SCENARIO — FICTIONAL TRAINING SCENARIO
Officer Torres responds to a domestic disturbance call at 214 Elm Street. Upon arrival, subject Marcus Webb, 34, is in the front yard holding a kitchen knife. Webb is approximately 15 feet away, yelling, and has not yet advanced toward the officer. A neighbor is standing on the adjacent porch, approximately 20 feet from Webb.
01
Assess the Graham factors immediately. Severity of crime — domestic disturbance, possible assault. Immediate threat — yes, subject is armed with a knife within potential closing distance. Actively resisting — not yet but verbal commands have not been issued. All three factors present. Use of force is potentially justified but not yet required.
02
De-escalation first. 15 feet with a stationary armed subject and a bystander present creates an opportunity to de-escalate. Officer Torres creates distance, calls for backup, issues clear verbal commands: "Sir, put the knife down. I want to help you." This is not weakness — it is tactically and legally sound. It also establishes the record of attempted de-escalation for any later report.
03
Document the threat assessment in real time mentally. If force becomes necessary, Officer Torres must be able to articulate: what the threat was, why the level of force used was proportional, what alternatives were considered, and what the outcome was. Starting that mental documentation now makes the Act 57 report accurate later.
04
If Webb advances. The calculus changes immediately. Armed subject closing distance with a knife — threat level escalates. The objective reasonableness standard asks: what would a reasonable officer in this situation believe? Lethal or intermediate force may now be justified under both Graham and PA § 508. The bystander's position matters — it affects backdrop considerations.
05
Post-incident — Act 57 reporting is mandatory. Whether or not force was used, if a weapon was displayed and verbal de-escalation commands were issued, document it. If force was used — any force, including pointing a firearm — reporting is required. Torres completes the department use of force form and the PSP report within the required timeframe. Failure to report is a separate policy violation.
The core principle: Every use of force decision must be objectively reasonable at the moment it was made — not in hindsight, not with information learned later. Document what you knew and perceived at the time.

STANDARD.

Five scenarios. Select the best response for each. Your answers are recorded in your training file.

Graham v. Connor
The three-factor test
During a post-incident review, a supervisor asks Officer Torres to justify the level of force used. Which three Graham v. Connor factors must be articulated?
Correct. The Graham three-factor test: severity of the crime at issue, whether the suspect poses an immediate threat to officers or others, and whether the suspect is actively resisting arrest or attempting to evade by flight. These three factors form the foundation of every use of force justification.
Incorrect. The Graham v. Connor three-factor test focuses on: severity of the crime, immediacy of threat to officers or others, and active resistance or attempt to flee. Criminal history, time of day, and supervisor approval are not part of the Graham standard — though they may be relevant to other analyses.
PA Title 18 § 508
Justified use of force
A subject actively resists arrest by pulling away and attempting to flee on foot. No weapon is visible. Which response best reflects Pennsylvania law on use of force in this situation?
Correct. Under PA § 508, non-deadly force may be used to effect a lawful arrest when the subject resists. Deadly force is only justified when the officer believes the subject presents a risk of death or serious bodily injury to the officer or others — not simply because the subject is fleeing. Tennessee v. Garner (1985) further restricts deadly force for fleeing suspects.
Incorrect. PA § 508 authorizes non-deadly force to effect a lawful arrest when there is resistance. Deadly force requires a belief that the subject poses a risk of death or serious bodily injury. A subject fleeing while unarmed does not meet the deadly force threshold. Unarmed does not mean force-free — it means the force level must be proportional.
De-escalation
The force continuum
Officer Chen arrives at a scene where a subject is agitated and verbally abusive but is not physically threatening anyone and has not committed a crime. What is the appropriate first response under Keystone PD policy?
Correct. When no crime has been committed and no immediate physical threat exists, the force continuum begins with officer presence and verbal communication. De-escalation is both a legal requirement and a tactical advantage — it creates a documented record of attempts to resolve the situation without force, which protects the officer if force later becomes necessary.
Incorrect. With no crime committed and no physical threat, the appropriate response is verbal de-escalation. Handcuffing without legal basis is unlawful detention. Displaying a firearm in a non-threatening situation is an unjustified escalation that could itself create a use of force incident. De-escalation is the required first step.
Act 57 of 2020
Reporting requirements
After an arrest involving physical force, Officer Torres secures the scene and transports the subject. At what point does Act 57 reporting become required, and what triggers it?
Correct. Act 57 of 2020 requires reporting for any use of physical force — not just force resulting in injury. This includes pointing a firearm at a subject. Reports go to the Pennsylvania State Police through the required reporting system. The low threshold is intentional — the law is designed to create a comprehensive record of force incidents statewide.
Incorrect. Act 57 reporting is triggered by any use of physical force during an arrest or detention — including pointing a firearm. Injury, complaints, and death are not prerequisites. Failure to report when required is a separate policy and potential legal violation. When in doubt, report.
Duty to Intervene
Another officer's use of force
Officer Torres witnesses a fellow officer applying a chokehold to a subject who is already handcuffed and not resisting. The subject is losing consciousness. What does the duty to intervene require?
Correct. The duty to intervene is an active legal obligation under Pennsylvania law and department policy. When an officer observes another using or about to use excessive force, they must physically intervene — not document later, not call a supervisor first, not issue a verbal warning and wait. A handcuffed non-resisting subject being choked is a clear excessive force situation requiring immediate physical intervention.
Incorrect. The duty to intervene is immediate and physical, not administrative. Waiting, documenting, calling supervisors, or deferring to the initiating officer are all insufficient responses when you are witnessing excessive force in real time. A handcuffed subject who is not resisting has no legal basis for force against them. Your obligation is to intervene now and report after.

Field application

CARRY THIS
ON DUTY.

Five principles. Apply them on every call from here forward.

01
Run the Graham factors before you act. Severity. Immediate threat. Active resistance. Those three questions take two seconds to answer and they are the foundation of every justification you will ever write.
02
De-escalate first when time and safety permit. It is not weakness. It is legally required, tactically sound, and it creates a documented record that protects you if force becomes necessary later.
03
Use force proportional to the threat — not the frustration. PA § 508 authorizes force necessary to effect a lawful arrest. The moment force exceeds what is necessary, it stops being lawful.
04
Report all force under Act 57 — when in doubt, report. The threshold is lower than most officers realize. Any physical force, including pointing a firearm, may trigger reporting requirements. Missing a report is a separate violation on top of anything else.
05
Intervene when you see excessive force. It is not optional, it is not a career decision, it is a legal obligation. Document what you did and when you did it.
